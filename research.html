Central Research Questions

Does architecting AI/ML to be more like human cognition improve performance? Why, or why not?

When we compare the fundamental processes of 'machine cognition', the binary electrical signal, and human cognition, the neuronal activation in the network that is our brain, we see a stark contrast. Machines lend themselves to tasks that are definable and repeatable, far outstretching standard human performance. However, some of the most basic operations of humans still seem to elude the power of machines. Even still, we have increasing evidence that architecting AI/ML to be more human-like improves performance on a variety of tasks.

Is intelligence an emergent property of architecture or a result of sufficiently robust data?

With the progress of multi-modality, we have seen great progress in AI/ML, yet we must not forget that such a boom was based on an architectural innovation, the transformer. We therefore have provided ourselves with two fronts on which to progress our understanding of intelligence, the two ways we 'actualize' intelligence: input and design; that is, training data and architectural difference in models. Indeed, the two domains of data and architecture may be inextricably linked: perhaps the architecture is capturing something fundamental about the data, and the data is an instance of something emergent from the architecture.

Research Philosophy

My research philosophy is born, naturally, of the path I have taken, and a reflection of the values I work to emulate:

  • Always be on Team Human: With perculating fears, founded and imaginiative, we are called, as investigators to prioritize humanity: in our aims, in our work, and especailly in our communicaiton. Further, we have a repsonsbility to use our insights to help Team Human overcome challanges, of all origins.
  • High-Fidelity Experimentation: With research questions like these, we delve into such subtle phenomena that only rigorous, high-fidelity experimentation can reveal. I draw upon my experiences in simulation, experimental design, and the rich background of cognitive scienc, as well as the study of the humanities when building out research projects.
  • ""Be curious, not judgmental" - Walt Whitman?" - Ted Lasso. Ours is not soley to adjudicate, but to educate, often beginning with ourselves.
